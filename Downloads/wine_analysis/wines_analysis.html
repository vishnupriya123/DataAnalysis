Scatterplot of the variables

Lets visualise the scatterplot of the above variables for good and bad wines.

Good wines are the ones with quality 7 and 8 Bad wines are the ones with quality 3 and 4

 qplot(data=subset(wines,(quality >6 | quality<5)),x=alcohol,y=pH,color=quality)+facet_wrap(~quality)

Good wines have higher alcohol content preferably > 12 and low pH values < 3.3 and bad wines have alcohol content < 10 and high pH values > 3.4

 qplot(data=subset(wines,(quality >6 | quality<5)),x=sulphates,y=fixed.acidity,color=quality) + facet_wrap(~quality)

Good wines have sulphates around 0.7 and above while bad ones have values around 0.5

qplot(data=subset(wines,(quality >6 | quality<5)),x=residual.sugar,y=fixed.acidity,color=quality)+facet_wrap(~quality)

Residual sugar and fixed acidity doesnt seem to vary much across good and bad ones

Scatterplot for pH vs Alchol
qplot(data=wines,x=alcohol,y=pH,color=quality) + facet_wrap(~quality)

Darker blues are towards lower side of the alcohol level and lighter blues are towards the higher side

Box plots

qplot(x = quality, y = alcohol, data = wines, geom = "boxplot", fill = as.factor(quality))

qplot(x = quality, y = pH, data = wines, geom = "boxplot", fill = as.factor(quality))

Alcohol strongly increases linearly as the quality increases while the pH weakly decreases linearly

Summary

A summary view of all the attributes of wine indicate that they do no vary significantly across various quality levels. This is evident from the histograms. Out of the 11 attributes, the most influencing once are alcohol and pH. They both vary linearly across the quality levels but in opposite direction.Out of the 2, Alcohol has a stronger impact on quality. More the alcohol, better is the quality
